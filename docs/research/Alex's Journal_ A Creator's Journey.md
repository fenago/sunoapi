# Alex's Journal: A Creator's Journey

---

## Entry One: Before Using the Product
### March 15th, 2:47 AM

I can't sleep again.

There's this melody looping in my head—has been for three days now. It's like a ghost that won't leave, haunting the space between my thoughts. I can *hear* it so clearly: this warm, pulsing bassline that feels like a heartbeat, layered with these shimmering synth pads that sound like city lights reflecting off wet pavement after rain. I can even hear where the vocal sample should come in, this chopped-up phrase that says something about "finding home in the noise."

But it's trapped. Locked inside my skull like a bird beating its wings against glass.

I opened Ableton tonight. Stared at that empty grid for forty-five minutes. The cursor just blinked at me, mocking. All those tracks, all those knobs and menus and routing options—it's like standing in front of a wall of a thousand locked doors and I don't have a single key. I clicked on "Operator" because I remembered some YouTube tutorial mentioning it, and this alien interface opened up with frequency modulation and oscillators and I just... closed my laptop.

My chest feels tight. That familiar knot of frustration sitting right below my ribs, getting heavier.

I scrolled Instagram instead. Watched this kid—can't be older than nineteen—post a video of his new track. 47,000 likes. Comments full of fire emojis and "this is a vibe" and producers asking to collab. His Spotify link in bio already has 12,000 monthly listeners. I felt my jaw clench so hard my teeth hurt. How? How does he just... *do* it? What does he know that I don't?

I'm 27. I have a degree. I make good money designing for clients who don't even remember my name. I can compose a visual hierarchy, I understand color theory, I can make a brand identity that makes people *feel* something. But I can't translate four bars of melody from my brain into a file I can actually play for someone.

The worst part? I know I'm capable. I *know* I have something to say. When I'm walking through the city with my headphones on, I can feel the rhythm of everything—the crosswalk signals, the coffee shop conversations, the way the light changes through the afternoon. It all has a tempo. It all wants to be music.

But there's this chasm between what I hear and what I can make real.

I have seventeen unfinished Ableton projects in my folder. Seventeen little graveyards of dead ideas. Each one starts with so much hope—"This is it, this is the one"—and ends with me staring at a loop I can't figure out how to extend, or a mix that sounds like it's playing through a tin can, or just... decision paralysis. Do I add a hi-hat? Is this the right key? Should there be more low end? Why does everyone else's music sound so *clean*?

My friend Maya asked me last week, "Hey, how's that music thing going?" and I felt my stomach drop. I mumbled something about "still learning the software" and changed the subject. She probably thinks I've given up. Maybe I have.

I'm angry. At myself, mostly. For quitting piano when I was ten because I thought scales were boring. For not starting this sooner. For being so easily intimidated. For watching tutorials instead of just making bad music until it gets better. For caring so much about what other people think.

But I'm also angry at the whole system. Why does it have to be this hard? Why do you need to understand signal flow and compression ratios and MIDI routing just to get an idea out of your head? Why does every forum post say "just put in your 10,000 hours" like I have that kind of time? I have client deadlines. I have rent. I have a life that's already happening while my music stays theoretical.

The melody is still there. Playing on loop. Mocking me.

I'm going to try to sleep. Maybe tomorrow I'll feel different. Maybe tomorrow I'll finally break through.

But probably not.

## Entry Two: During Their First Use of Product
### March 22nd, 11:38 PM

Okay. Okay, something just happened.

My hands are actually shaking as I write this. I need to get this down before the feeling fades.

Benn Jordan posted a video today about this new platform—some kind of AI music creation thing that's also a social network? I almost scrolled past it because I'm so tired of "revolutionary" tools that are just repackaged loop libraries. But he seemed genuinely excited, not sponsored-excited. He said something like, "This is what I wish existed when I was starting out."

So I signed up. Free tier, obviously. Low expectations.

The interface loaded and I just... stopped. It wasn't Ableton's intimidating spaceship cockpit. It wasn't FL Studio's overwhelming everything-everywhere-all-at-once. It was clean. Almost too simple. A text box that said: "Describe the music in your head."

I laughed out loud. Like, actually laughed. It felt ridiculous.

But then I thought about that melody. The one that's been haunting me. And I just... started typing.

*"A warm, pulsing bassline that feels like a heartbeat. Shimmering synth pads like city lights on wet pavement. Chopped vocal sample saying 'finding home in the noise.' Lo-fi hip-hop meets ambient electronica. Introspective. Late night drive through an empty city."*

I hit generate. Watched this little progress bar fill up. My heart was pounding—that same feeling I get right before a client presentation, that mix of hope and dread.

And then it played.

I'm not going to lie and say it was exactly what I heard in my head. It wasn't. But it was *close*. Close enough that I felt this jolt of electricity run through my entire body. The bassline was there—not identical, but the *feeling* was right. The pads had that shimmer. There was even a vocal chop, though it said something different.

But here's the thing: *it existed*. The idea that lived only in my head for three days was now a real sound wave moving through air, hitting my eardrums, making my chest vibrate.

I played it again. And again. I closed my eyes and just listened.

Then I saw the edit button. I could extend it. Change the style. Add a section. Remix it. And—this is the part that made my breath catch—I could see other people's creations. Real people, making real music, right now. There was a chat. A community. Someone named @midnightproducer had just made something similar and people were commenting, encouraging, collaborating.

I felt something I haven't felt in months: possibility.

Not the crushing weight of "you need to learn this entire technical skillset before you're allowed to create." Just... possibility. Like the door I've been throwing myself against finally cracked open.

I made three more tracks tonight. Three. In two hours. Are they perfect? No. Are they "professional"? Definitely not. But they're *mine*. They came from my brain, through this platform, and into reality.

One of them—this ambient piece I called "2AM Thoughts"—I posted to the platform. Within ten minutes, someone commented: "This is beautiful. Exactly what I needed tonight." 

I read that comment five times. Then I cried. Just sat here at my desk with tears running down my face because someone *heard* me. Not my design work, not my carefully curated Instagram aesthetic, but the actual music that lives in my chest.

The frustration is still there. I still don't understand compression or EQ or any of that technical stuff. But for the first time, I don't feel like that's a wall I can't climb. It feels like... a skill I can learn *while* I'm creating, not *before* I'm allowed to create.

I'm not trapped anymore.

The melody is out. It's real. And there are a thousand more where that came from.

I need to make more. Right now.

## Entry Three: After Using The Product
### April 8th, 7:15 PM

I've been on the platform for two weeks and I think something fundamental has shifted in me.

I'm sitting in my favorite coffee shop right now, the one with the big windows that face west so the afternoon light turns everything golden. There's this ambient hum of conversation and espresso machines and someone's laptop playing lo-fi beats. Six months ago, I would have felt that familiar ache—that longing to be part of the music world, to contribute to the soundtrack, but feeling locked out.

Now? I'm listening differently. I'm listening like someone who *makes* things.

I have twelve finished tracks now. Twelve. I've made more music in two weeks than in the previous two years of "trying to learn Ableton." And here's what's wild: they're getting better. Not because I suddenly understand music theory or learned how to use a compressor (though I'm starting to pick that up through the platform's community tutorials), but because I'm *doing* it. Every day. The act of creation is teaching me.

My radio station—"Late Night Transmissions"—has 247 followers. Two hundred and forty-seven people chose to listen to my curated space. Some of them are other creators I've connected with. Some are just listeners who vibe with the aesthetic. We have this little community now. We comment on each other's tracks, we collaborate, we share samples and ideas.

Last night, this producer from Berlin—@klangfarbe—reached out about remixing one of my tracks. Me. Someone wants to remix *my* music. We're talking about splitting the rights through the platform's fractional ownership system. I might actually make money from this. Not life-changing money, but enough to cover my subscription and maybe buy some better headphones.

But honestly? The money isn't even the point.

The point is that I wake up excited now. I have this ritual: coffee, journal, then thirty minutes of creation before I even look at client emails. Sometimes I'm building on yesterday's idea. Sometimes I'm starting fresh. Sometimes I'm just messing around with sounds, learning, playing.

The anxiety is still there sometimes. I still compare myself to others. I still have moments where I listen to a track I made and think, "This isn't good enough." But it's different now. Before, that voice would stop me completely. Now, it's just... part of the process. I post it anyway. I get feedback. I make the next one better.

I ran into Maya yesterday. She asked about the music thing again, but this time I didn't feel that stomach-drop of shame. I pulled out my phone and played her "2AM Thoughts." Watched her face as she listened. She got quiet, then looked at me and said, "Alex, this is really beautiful. I didn't know you had this in you."

I wanted to tell her that it was always in me. It was just trapped. But now I have a way to let it out.

The envy I used to feel when I saw other creators succeeding? It's transforming into something else. Inspiration, maybe. Or solidarity. We're all in this together, trying to translate the invisible into the audible. The platform makes it feel less like a competition and more like a conversation.

I'm planning my first live set. Just a small thing—a friend's art gallery opening next month. I'll play my tracks, maybe do some live manipulation, create a vibe. The old me would have said, "I'm not ready. I need more time. I need to be better first." But I'm learning that readiness is a myth. You just start.

There's this moment that keeps happening: I'll be walking through the city, or lying in bed, or sitting here in this golden-hour coffee shop, and a melody will arrive. But now, instead of that crushing frustration of knowing I can't capture it, I feel this quiet confidence. I'll get home, I'll open the platform, and I'll make it real.

The bird isn't trapped behind glass anymore.

It's flying.

And I'm flying with it.
